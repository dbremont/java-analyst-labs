Using the Log Intelligence View

This guide explains how to navigate the interface, filter log streams, and interpret the analytics charts to debug issues faster.

The Stream Viewer

The Stream Viewer is the default tab. It acts like a "live tail" of your server logs, allowing you to search and filter through thousands of entries in real-time.

1

The Search Bar

Located at the top, the search bar lets you perform full-text search across all log messages.

  • • Simple Search: Type a word like timeout to find all logs containing that term.
  • • Exact Phrase: Use quotes like "connection refused" to find that exact string.
Tip: The search is instant. It updates the list automatically as you type.
2

Level Filters

Use the filter buttons below the stats cards to narrow down logs by severity.

INFO DEBUG WARN ERROR

Clicking ERROR will hide everything except error messages, making it easy to spot critical failures.

3

Reading an Entry

Each log entry is formatted for readability:

2025-02-15 10:32:45 ERROR [com.app.Service]
Failed to connect to database.
  • Timestamp: When the event happened.
  • Badge: The severity level (Info, Warn, Error).
  • Message: The actual log content.
4

Loading History

The view loads the most recent logs first. To go further back in time, click the "Load Older Logs" button at the bottom of the list.

The Analytics Dashboard

Switch to the Analytics tab to see the "big picture". This view helps you identify trends, spikes in traffic, and recurring patterns without reading individual lines.

Time Resolution (Important)

In the top right of the Analytics tab, you will see the Resolution selector: 1m 5m 15m 30m 1h.

This controls how time is "collapsed" in the charts.

High Resolution (1m)

Shows a data point for every minute. Use this to pinpoint the exact minute a server crashed or spiked. The chart will be very wide and requires horizontal scrolling.

Low Resolution (1h)

Groups all logs within an hour into one point. Use this to see daily traffic patterns. The chart fits on the screen easily.

Interacting with Charts

The charts are interactive. Move your mouse over the chart area:

  • Crosshair: A vertical line appears to help you align values.
  • Tooltip: A box shows the exact count and time for that point.
  • Scroll: If you select "1m" resolution, the chart becomes very wide. Scroll left/right to navigate through history.

Term Frequency Cloud

This visualizes the most common words found in your logs.

  • Size: Larger words appear more frequently.
  • Click: Clicking a word (e.g., "failed") automatically switches you back to the Stream Viewer and searches for that term. This is a fast way to investigate recurring errors.

Detected Patterns

Below the charts, you will see two lists:

Unique Identifiers

A list of UUIDs found in the logs. Useful for tracking specific request flows.

Form Contexts

Shows which form contexts are logging the most activity.

Data Scale Reference

When you see numbers like "48,291 log entries" or a chart spike at "3,200/min", what does that actually mean? This section gives you the mental yardsticks to interpret log data at a glance.

Storage & Characters

Text Capacity
1 Byte = 1 ASCII character (letter, number, space) 1 char
1 Kilobyte (KB) = 1,024 characters ~1,024 chars
1 Megabyte (MB) = 1,048,576 characters ~1.05M chars
1 Gigabyte (GB) = 1,073,741,824 characters ~1.07B chars
Log Lines Per Unit
Typical log line (timestamp + level + class + message) ~120–200 chars
Lines in 1 KB ~5–8 lines
Lines in 1 MB ~5,000–8,000
Lines in 1 GB ~5–8 million
Why this matters: If your log file is 250 MB, you're holding roughly 1.2–2 million lines. No human can read that. That's exactly why the search bar and filters exist — they are not a convenience, they are a necessity.

Throughput & Time

How Fast Does Data Pile Up?
10 logs/sec for 1 hour 36,000 lines
100 logs/sec for 1 hour 360,000 lines
1,000 logs/sec for 1 hour 3.6 million
10,000 logs/sec for 1 hour 36 million
Time to Fill 1 MB at Various Rates
1 log/sec (quiet service) ~83–133 min
100 logs/sec (moderate load) ~50–80 sec
1,000 logs/sec (high traffic) ~5–8 sec
10,000 logs/sec (peak burst) ~0.5–0.8 sec
Why this matters: A chart spike showing "12,400 logs in one minute" means roughly 200 logs/second. If your baseline is 20/sec, that's a 10× spike — not just "more logs" but an order-of-magnitude change that usually indicates an error storm or a retry loop.

Human Reading Limits

Screen & Scrolling
Visible lines on a typical 1080p monitor (log viewer) ~25–35 lines
Pages to scroll through 10,000 lines ~285–400 pages
Time at 1 page/sec (aggressive skimming) ~5–7 minutes
Time at 5 sec/line (actually reading) ~14 hours
Reading Speed Benchmarks
Average adult reading prose ~250 words/min
Scanning log lines (not prose — structured data) ~12–20 lines/min
Time to read 1,000 log lines carefully ~50–83 minutes
Why this matters: If an incident generated 15,000 error lines, reading them all would take 12–20 hours. The correct workflow is: filter to ERROR → search for a unique identifier → read only the 10–30 lines that matter. The tools are designed to reduce hours of reading to seconds of searching.

Chart Resolution Reference

Data Points Per Time Range
1m resolution over 1 hour 60 points
1m resolution over 24 hours 1,440 points
5m resolution over 24 hours 288 points
15m resolution over 24 hours 96 points
1h resolution over 7 days 168 points
1h resolution over 30 days 720 points
What Resolution Should You Use?
"When exactly did the crash happen?" 1m
"Is traffic higher on weekdays?" 1h
"Did the deployment at 14:30 cause issues?" 5m
"Overall trend this month?" 1h
Why this matters: At 1m over 24 hours you get 1,440 bars on the chart. That's too many to see at once — the chart becomes a solid block of color. Switch to 5m (288 bars) or 15m (96 bars) to see the shape of the data. High resolution is for zooming in on a specific hour, not for the full day.

Network & Transfer

How Long to Transfer...
1 MB log file on a 50 Mbps connection ~0.16 sec
100 MB log file on a 50 Mbps connection ~16 sec
1 GB log file on a 50 Mbps connection ~2.7 min
1 GB log file on a 5 Mbps connection (slow VPN) ~27 min
JSON Overhead
Raw log line (plain text) ~150 chars
Same line as JSON (with field names, quotes, braces) ~250–350 chars
JSON overhead factor ~1.7–2.3×
Why this matters: If you're loading logs over a VPN and the page feels slow, it's likely not the app — it's the 2× size penalty of JSON combined with limited bandwidth. The "Load Older Logs" button fetches in small batches (typically 200–500 rows) specifically to avoid this problem.

Quick Mental Math for Logs

Memorize these rough conversions and you can estimate anything on the fly:

Rule of Thumb
1 MB ≈ 6,000 lines
Good enough for back-of-napkin math. Multiply file size in MB by 6K.
Rule of Thumb
100 logs/sec ≈ 1 MB/min
If your rate display shows ~100/s, you're generating roughly a megabyte per minute.
Rule of Thumb
1 screen ≈ 30 lines
Divide total lines by 30 to get "how many screens to scroll" — grounds the number.
Rule of Thumb
10× spike = investigate now
A 2–3× change might be normal variance. 10× almost always means something broke.

Quick Glossary

FTS

Full-Text Search. The technology powering the instant search bar.

Bucket

A time window (like 5 minutes) where logs are grouped together for charts.

Keyset Pagination

The method used to load older logs efficiently without slowing down the page.

Logger

The class or component in your code that generated the log message.
